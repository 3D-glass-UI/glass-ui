The raison d'être

What on earth is this? And why did I torture myself making it?

After picking my jaw up off the floor post-Apple Vision Pro keynote, I was struck with the inescapable urge to find out if that exquisite spatial UI could be rendered using CSS. The answer wasn't obvious. After all, the UI is meant to live on a unique piece of hardware, running on a custom operating system. For all I knew, the whole R1 chip might just be there to run real-time ray tracing for a UI that’s constructed from actual 3D meshes. I jest, but only a little.

So, why does this website exist, you might ask?

  • To share a detailed 3D glass tutorial (SCSS).
  • To demo the open-sourced UI library I ended up writing (and rewriting, and rewriting...).
  • And, to chronicle everything that didn’t work, to save you the bother.

The temporal context

Glassmorphism's epic journey

Surprisingly (at least to me), glassmorphism dates back to the early years of this millennium. Take Project Looking Glass, for example. Developed in 2003, this was an entire desktop environment designed to resemble thick glass slabs. Usable? Not so much. Impressive? Absolutely. Hideya Kawahara was designing two full decades ahead of his time. Available compute power and rendering resources be damned!

Over the course of those two decades, glassmorphism has ridden the rollercoaster of popularity. From the moderate heights of Apple's iOS 7 to the irredeemable depths of Windows Vista, this design trend has seen it all. Now, with the Apple Vision Pro, hitting the scene, I predict a new peak for glassmorphism on our digital horizons.

The aesthetic ideal

What makes this glass the fairest of them all?

Take a close look at Vision Pro’s UI, and you'll notice it's not your run-of-the-mill glassmorphism. Yet, at first glance, it's difficult to pinpoint exactly what sets it apart. Much like the rest of everything Apple ever does, it just seems subliminally superior and more polished. Consequently, I spent a rather not-so-insignificant amount of time capturing screenshots from the Vision Pro keynote and wishing that yelling "enhance!" at my computer would actually yield results. Here are my findings:

Key characteristics

  • A well-defined, yet subtle, beveled edge.
  • Dazzling specular highlights.
  • A very wide blur radius.
  • An extremely fine-grained noise texture.
  • Some sort of blend mode magic that adds a dynamic depth of color to the UI as the backdrop behind it changes. Disclaimer: this technique doesn't play nice with all browsers. For the full saga of my trials and tribulations, check out the tutorial page.

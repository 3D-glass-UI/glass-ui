Glassmorphism gets a glowup

Surprisingly (at least to me), glassmorphism dates back to the early years of this millennium. Developed in 2003, Project Looking Glass was an entire desktop environment designed to resemble slabs of glass. From what I understand, it was rather unusable. However, I remain impressed; Hideya Kawahara was essentially designing two full decades ahead of his time.

Since then, glassmorphism has experienced several peaks and troughs in popularity. However, now that Apple has unveiled the Vision Pro, I foresee an upcoming high point for glassmorphism on our digital horizons.

What makes Vision Pro’s UI so distinctive?

Upon close examination, one may notice that Vision Pro’s UI doesn't quite resemble any of the existing glassmorphism designs. It's difficult to pinpoint exactly what sets it apart, but it appears more polished and dimensional. Consequently, I spent a rather significant amount of time capturing screenshots from the Vision Pro keynote and wishing that yelling "enhance!" at my computer would actually yield results. Here are my findings:

Key characteristics

  • A well-defined, yet subtle, beveled edge.
  • Dazzling specular highlights.
  • A very large blur radius.
  • An extremely fine-grained noise texture.
  • Some sort of blend mode magic that adds dynamic depth of color to the UI. After much experimentation, I've found that this doesn't consistently work for the web (yet). For a detailed account of my trials and tribulations, refer to the end of the tutorial section.

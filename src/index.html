The raison d'être

What is this and why did I make it?

After retrieving the jaw I had dropped during the Apple Vision Pro keynote, I was struck with the inescapable need to figure out if that exquisite spatial UI could be rendered using CSS. The answer wasn't an obvious one since the UI is to live on one-of-a-kind hardware using a custom operating system. For all I know the whole R1 chip is really just there to run real-time ray tracing for UI that’s constructed from actual 3D meshes. I jest, but only a little.

This website exists...

  • to share a detailed 3D glass tutorial (SCSS)
  • to demo the open-sourced UI library I ended up writing
  • to chronicle everything that didn’t work, to save you the bother

The temporal context

Glassmorphism gets a glow-up

Surprisingly (at least to me), glassmorphism dates back to the early years of this millennium. For instance, Project Looking Glass (developed in 2003) was an entire desktop environment designed to resemble slabs of glass. From what I understand, it was rather unusable. However, I remain impressed; Hideya Kawahara was essentially designing two full decades ahead of his time.

Over the course of those two decades, glassmorphism has experienced several peaks and troughs in popularity. From Apple's iOS 7 (released in 2013) to “Acrylic" in Microsoft's fluent design system. And now that Apple has unveiled the Vision Pro, I foresee an imminent high point for glassmorphism on our digital horizons.

The aesthetic ideal

What makes Vision Pro’s UI so special?

Upon close examination, one may notice that Vision Pro’s UI doesn't quite resemble any of the existing glassmorphism designs. It's difficult to pinpoint exactly what sets it apart, but it appears more polished and dimensional. Consequently, I spent a rather significant amount of time capturing screenshots from the Vision Pro keynote and wishing that yelling "enhance!" at my computer would actually yield results. Here are my findings:

Key characteristics

  • A well-defined, yet subtle, beveled edge.
  • Dazzling specular highlights.
  • A very large blur radius.
  • An extremely fine-grained noise texture.
  • Some sort of blend mode magic that adds dynamic depth of color to the UI as the backdrop behind it changes. After much experimentation, I've found that this technique doesn't reliably work across browsers. For a detailed account of my trials and tribulations, check out the tutorial page.

The end product

Okay, so how is this done?

I combined backdrop-filters, a complex box-shadow layering technique, and a super fine noise texture to create a glass effect so realistic, you'll be trying to wipe your nose-print off the screen.
